Slide 1 — Title · [0:00]

[Breathe. Look at the room.]

Thank you. I've come from San Juan, a province in the west of Argentina, on the periphery of the health system. I'd like to tell you how we are building dementia care there — before and after diagnosis — by treating lived experience and local knowledge as evidence.

Slide 2 — The problem, with a face · [0:20]

[Lower your voice. This is a person.]

It is three in the morning. A caregiver once told me: "I don't know how to respond. Seeing her like this, I break down."

[Pause.] To one single talk, two hundred and ten caregivers signed up. So the most expert decision about dementia tonight, in my province, is being made by one of them — alone, at three in the morning.

[Pause.] That is the after of diagnosis. But most people are never diagnosed at all. So let us begin with the before.

Slide 3 — The path · [1:10]

This is the whole story in a single line. On the left, everything we built so that people would reach a diagnosis. In the middle, the day the Unit opened. On the right, what we learned afterwards. And notice the loop: it returns to the beginning — because lived experience came back to redesign the system. Let me walk you through it.

Slide 4 — Neuromentia · [1:50]

We began by measuring. With the Catholic University of Cuyo we built Neuromentia, the programme our team leads, and we assessed more than eight hundred people in the community. From the clinic, we could not see this: the risk was concentrated in the periphery, and a great many people had never been diagnosed. The problem was outside — out where we were not looking.

Slide 5 — Prevention · [2:40]

[Hope, not catastrophe.] Here is the hopeful part. The Lancet Commission says that around forty-five per cent of dementia is potentially modifiable. We recalculated it with our own data, and in San Juan it comes to roughly sixty-one per cent. Nearly two in three cases. And what weighs most — education, blood pressure, obesity — can be acted on without a neurologist. This is brain health that lives in the community, not in the clinic.

Slide 6 — The territory · [3:25]

Now the uncomfortable part. Where cognitive impairment is greatest, the neurologists aren't. Measured with Bruno's cut-off, adjusted for education, the risk is higher in the periphery: thirty-nine against twenty-seven. It is not that we mismeasure the poor. The inequality is real, and the system filters it the wrong way round.

Slide 7 — Equity · [4:10]

So the periphery has a higher ceiling to prevent — sixty-nine per cent against sixty-one in the centre — and a different profile. In the city, education weighs most. In the periphery, it is cardiometabolic and behavioural: obesity, blood pressure, smoking, inactivity. The periphery is not the place that matters least. It is the place with the most to gain.

Slide 8 — Measurement · [4:55]

Cognition is carved by the social. Between the highest and the lowest education there are nearly twenty-nine points on the ACE-three cognitive scale — an enormous social gap. That is why we measure with local norms, adjusted for education, not with imported cut-offs. The local norms do exist. The problem is not that they are missing; it is that imported ones are still being used.

Slide 9 — MRCA · [5:35]

[Builder's tone.] We had the data, but the community health workers were not using the platform. It was too long. So we rebuilt it with them: short, self-reported, usable — a triage for the pre-consultation, where there is no neurologist. And let me be honest: in validation it falls to zero point six three. It is only marginally better than age plus education, and not significantly so. It does not need to be. Its value is the equitable rule-out, and that a health worker can actually use it. It replaces no one.

Slide 10 — The hinge · [6:30]

[This is the turn.] And so the demand was there, and the Memory Unit was opened — pushed by the risk we were seeing before consultation, and by all the undiagnosed people that Neuromentia and ReDLat had uncovered. Ninety-two appointments in the first year. And the lesson of that first year is simple: the barrier is awareness. The need is enormous, yet most people are never detected, or never referred. Here, at last, diagnosis begins.

Slide 11 — The caregiver · [7:15]

And on the other side, something else was waiting. The problem of the after was support. The burden is behavioural — sixty-three per cent of families face three or more difficult behaviours at once. We ran a workshop. It was not enough — because the need is at three in the morning, anywhere. That is where technology is the opportunity. So the Caregiver Guide was born — free, and offline, in co-creation, and we are seeking partners to validate it. You can scan it now. [Wait 3–4 s.]

Slide 12 — The PRE / POST system · [8:10]

[Point to the network.] Look at the change. In twenty twenty-three, the system was a single point — the central clinic. Today it points towards the whole province, as a network we are building. A pre-diagnosis agent: screening, the Lancet risk factors, polypharmacy. A post-diagnosis agent: the caregiver. And the human health worker as the node that connects the centre to the home, with digital tools as the vehicle. It is a prototype, co-designed with people living with dementia, in development — scan the code and help us shape it. Built with ethics: minimal data, and it never replaces the human.

Slide 13 — The connectors · [9:00]

Why a connector, and not simply a better test? Because the evidence shows it shortens diagnostic delay, prevents admissions, sustains the caregiver, and reaches where the system does not. The evidence never asked for a better test. It asked for a system. And policy should fund what is co-designed — as Chile did, bringing dementia into primary care through its National Dementia Plan, driven by GBHI Atlantic Fellows.

Slide 14 — Close · [9:40]

[Slow down.] We began with a caregiver, alone at three in the morning. Before and after diagnosis, the answer is one single, person-centred network — woven from tools co-created with the very people who use them.

[Beat.] And as Cindy said at Walking the Talk for Dementia last year: nothing about us, without us.

[Pause.] So let me leave you with a question. What knowledge are we ignoring — or treating as ignorance — when it is, in fact, lived experience that should guide co-design?

[Pause.] Thank you.
