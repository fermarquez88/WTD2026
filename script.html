Before and After Diagnosis

Updated · strategic cut · ~7–8 min · Fernando Márquez, MD
Full where your voice is the content (slides 2, 5, 9, 10, 11, 14); trimmed where the slide already shows the numbers or the map (3, 6, 8, 13) — point, don't recite. Grey italics in [brackets] are delivery cues. A− / A+ changes size and remembers it.

Slide 1 — Title · [0:00] · full

[Breathe. Look at the room.]

Thank you. I've come from San Juan, a province in the west of Argentina, on the periphery of the health system. I'd like to tell you how we are building dementia care there — before and after diagnosis — by treating lived experience and local knowledge as evidence.

Slide 2 — The problem, with a face · [0:25] · full

[Lower your voice. This is a person.]

It is three in the morning. A caregiver once told me: "I don't know how to respond. Seeing her like this, I break down."

[Optional — only if you want to share it. It is the most powerful version, but it is yours to give: That caregiver was my mother, Ivonne. The woman she cared for, my grandmother, María Rosario, living with dementia. This is where my work begins.]

[Pause.] To the first talk we held at our Memory Unit — the first, not the only one — two hundred and ten caregivers signed up. So the most expert decision about dementia tonight, in my province, is being made by one of them — alone, at three in the morning.

[Pause.] That is the after of diagnosis. But most people are never diagnosed at all. So let us begin with the before.

Slide 3 — The path · [1:25] · pointer

[Gesture across the arc — don't narrate each box.] This is the whole story in a single line. And notice the loop: it returns to the beginning, because lived experience came back to redesign the system.

Slide 4 — Neuromentia · [1:45] · keep

We began by measuring. With the Catholic University of Cuyo we built Neuromentia, and we assessed more than eight hundred people in the community. From the clinic, we could not see this: the risk was concentrated in the periphery, and a great many had never been diagnosed. The problem was outside — out where we were not looking.

Slide 5 — Prevention · [2:20] · full

[Hope, not catastrophe.] Here is the hopeful part. The Lancet Commission says that, globally, around forty-five per cent of dementia is potentially modifiable. We recalculated it with our own data, and in San Juan it comes to sixty-one per cent — nearly two in three cases. And what weighs most can be acted on without a neurologist. This is brain health that lives in the community, not in the clinic.

Slide 6 — The territory · [2:55] · pointer

[Point to the map.] Now the uncomfortable part. Where cognitive impairment is greatest, the neurologists aren't. The queue filters in reverse.

Slide 7 — Equity · [3:15] · keep

So the periphery has a higher ceiling to prevent, and a different profile: in the city, education weighs most; in the periphery, it is cardiometabolic and behavioural. The periphery is not the place that matters least. It is the place with the most to gain.

Slide 8 — Measurement · [3:40] · pointer

And cognition is carved by the social — the gap on that scale is education, not disease. That is why we measure with local norms, not imported cut-offs.

Slide 9 — MRCA · [4:00] · full

[Builder's tone.] We had the data, but the health workers weren't using the platform — it was too long. So we rebuilt it with them: short, self-reported, usable. And let me be honest: it is only marginally better than age plus education, and not significantly so. It doesn't need to be. Its value is the equitable rule-out, and that a health worker can actually use it. It replaces no one.

Slide 10 — The hinge · [4:35] · full

[This is the turn.] And so the demand was there, and the Memory Unit was opened — pushed by the risk we saw before consultation, and by all the undiagnosed people that Neuromentia and ReDLat had uncovered. Ninety-two appointments in the first year. And the lesson is simple: the barrier is awareness — and the lack of a network. Here, at last, diagnosis begins.

Slide 11 — The caregiver · [5:10] · full

And on the other side, something else was waiting. The problem of the after was support, and the burden is behavioural. We ran a workshop — it was not enough, because the need is at three in the morning, anywhere. That is where technology is the opportunity. So the Caregiver Guide was born — free, offline, in co-creation, and we are seeking partners to validate it. You can scan it now. [Wait 3–4 s.]

Slide 12 — The PRE / POST system · [5:45] · keep

[Point to the network.] Look at the change. In twenty twenty-three, one point — the central clinic. Today, a network we are building across the province: a pre-diagnosis agent, a post-diagnosis agent, and the human health worker as the node that connects the centre to the home. A prototype, co-designed with people living with dementia. Scan the code and help us shape it.

Slide 13 — The connectors · [6:20] · pointer

Why a connector, and not simply a better test? Because — as you can see — it shortens delay, prevents admissions, sustains the caregiver, and reaches where the system does not. The evidence never asked for a better test. It asked for a system. And policy should fund what is co-designed — as Chile did, driven by GBHI Atlantic Fellows.

Slide 14 — Close · [6:50] · full

[Slow down.] We began with a caregiver, alone at three in the morning. Before and after diagnosis, the answer is one single, person-centred network — woven from tools co-created with the very people who use them.

[Beat.] And as Cindy said at Walking the Talk for Dementia last year: nothing about us, without us.

[Pause.] So let me leave you with a question. What knowledge are we ignoring — or treating as ignorance — when it is, in fact, lived experience that should guide co-design?

[Pause.] Thank you.
